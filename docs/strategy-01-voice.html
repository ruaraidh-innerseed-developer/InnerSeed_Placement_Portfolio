01

Why voice comes first

A channel plan without a fixed voice just moves inconsistency faster. Before Part Two maps where the brand shows up, this document fixes what it sounds like when it does — so an email, an App Store description, and a founder interview transcript all read as the same person talking, even when three different people are writing them.

The raw material for that voice already exists. It was recorded, not invented: in the tone Jeroen used across five weeks of focus calls, in the specific language he reached for when the conversation moved from bug fixes to ambition — "let the imagination run its due course," "what will sell, what can be developed" — and in the three words he named as the philosophy the whole app should be read through: Root, Rise, Return. This document's job is to notice that voice was already there and write it down before three different content writers each invent a different one.

02

The tone-of-voice guide

Four attributes, each stated as a spectrum rather than a single word, because a spectrum is falsifiable and a word is not:

Grounded, not mysticalBreathwork writing defaults to abstraction fast. InnerSeed Garden's advantage in the audit was the opposite instinct — Jeroen wanted the app read through Root, Rise, Return as a structure, not a vibe. The copy should be able to say what a session actually does before it says how it might make you feel.

Confident, not hypedThe Spotify import finding (D-43) is a genuine, named commercial differentiator — no mainstream competitor in the category offers equivalent personal playlist import. That's stated as fact, once, clearly. It doesn't need "revolutionary" in front of it. Overclaiming a real advantage reads as insecurity about it.

Precise, not clinicalThe audit's own method — raw first, structured second — is a voice lesson as much as a research method. Specificity ("a session you can finish in the time it takes the kettle to boil") beats a general wellness adjective every time. Precision is warmer than people expect; vagueness is what reads cold.

Quietly expert, not lecturingNielsen and Norman sit underneath the Manifestos; they never sit on top of a marketing page. The same discipline applies here — cite evidence in an internal brief, never in front of a user. A user should feel the app was thought through, not be told it was.

03

Message hierarchy — proof before aspiration

Every channel in Part Two, every template in Part Three, pulls from the same three-tier stack. Higher tiers can be dropped for space; lower tiers can't be promoted above them.

TierSaysGrounded in
1 — ProofWhat the app already, demonstrably does wellD-43 (Spotify import), D-48 (session share card), the Root/Rise/Return session structure itself
2 — CraftHow it was built and refinedThreefold consistency (D-21), the consolidated language fix (D-03) — evidence of a product taken seriously, stated as fact not as reassurance
3 — HorizonWhere it could goThe Garden Lab Proposal territory — spatial computing, AI-guided practice, retreat programming. Always labelled as direction, never sold as a current feature.

Tier 3 is the one most likely to get promoted too early by an eager campaign. It's fenced off here on purpose: aspiration sells once and then has to be walked back; proof compounds.

04

Root, Rise, Return — the brand's throughline

Jeroen named Root, Rise, Return directly, in the 18 June focus call, as the philosophy he wanted future development read through. It is not a marketing invention layered on afterward — it is already the shape of a Builder session, and it is the same spine this whole portfolio site borrows for its own structure. That's not a coincidence; it's the throughline doing its job in a second place.

  • Root — orientation, arrival, the settling-in a session (or a first-time user) needs before anything else works. In copy: what the app is, plainly, before what it can become.
  • Rise — the built work itself: Builder mode, the session structure, the findings turned into fixes. In copy: proof and craft, tiers one and two.
  • Return — closing the loop, carrying something forward. In copy: the invitation back — save, share, come again — never a hard sell.

Used this way, Root/Rise/Return is a checklist as much as a metaphor: does this piece of content orient, build proof, or invite return — and does it know which one it's doing?

05

The Enschede founder interview — brief & shot list

Agreed with Jeroen as cornerstone content for the Brand Book, filmed in his studio in Enschede. This is the single asset every other content decision in Parts Two and Four cuts down from — so it needs to be shot once, properly, rather than revisited.

GoalCapture the voice this document describes at its source, in Jeroen's own words, so future copywriters have something to match rather than a style guide alone.

Core questionsWhy Garden, why now; what Root, Rise, Return means to him in his own words; the story behind one real feature (Spotify import is the obvious choice — it's already proven, it has a good origin story, and it under-sells nothing); what he'd say to someone opening the app for the first time.

Tone on cameraSame four attributes as the copy: grounded, confident without hype, specific, quietly expert. Direct address to camera, studio setting, minimal production — the space itself (his studio) is part of the story, not a backdrop to hide.

Deliverable cut-downsOne long-form piece (3–5 min, Brand Book anchor); three 30–45 second cut-downs, one per message tier above; a transcript, filed alongside this document, that future writers can quote from directly rather than paraphrase.

07

What this hands off

Part Two (Channel & Content Framework) takes the three-tier message stack and decides where each tier is said and how often. Part Three (the Sales Development Process Manual) borrows Tier 1 wholesale — Spotify import and the session share card are the two moments doing the actual convincing at the point of conversion. Part Four's editorial calendar exists to keep the founder interview's cut-downs and the voice attributes above consistent as the volume of content scales past what one person is writing by hand.

Nothing in Parts Two through Five should introduce a tone, a claim, or a piece of language that isn't traceable back to a rule in this document. If it is, this document is wrong and should be corrected — not quietly overridden downstream.
